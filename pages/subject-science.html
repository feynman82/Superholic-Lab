MOE Science Curriculum Framework

Five Themes. Every Topic in Primary Science Belongs to One.

MOE organises the entire P3–P6 Science curriculum across five themes. Understanding the theme behind each topic helps children connect ideas across years — a key skill that PSLE open-ended questions test explicitly.

Diversity

Living vs non-living things, types of animals and plants, classification, and diversity of materials. Builds the ability to observe, classify, and compare systematically.

P3 · Foundation theme
Cycles

Life cycles of animals and plants, the water cycle, states of matter, and reproduction. Understanding that patterns of change repeat helps students predict and explain natural events — a core PSLE skill.

P3–P5 · Spans 3 levels
Systems

Plant systems, human digestive, respiratory, and circulatory systems, and electrical systems. A "system" is a set of parts that work together — questions always ask how each part's function contributes to the whole.

P4–P5 · High mark weightage
Interactions

Forces (magnets at P3, frictional/gravitational/elastic spring forces at P6), food chains and webs, adaptation, and man's impact on the environment. Connects physics concepts to ecological understanding.

P3 + P6 · Forces are PSLE favourites
Energy

Forms and uses of light and heat at P4, photosynthesis and energy conversion at P5. Energy questions require students to trace how energy transforms — from sunlight to chemical energy to movement.

P4–P5 · Energy conversion
Level-by-Level Syllabus

What Changes at Every Level.

From 2024, each primary level has its own dedicated Science syllabus — there is no longer a shared "lower block." Every school teaches the same topics in the same sequence.

P3
First Year of Science

Science is a new subject at P3 — and with it comes a new way of thinking and writing answers. The vocabulary of classification, characteristics, and functions must be learnt from the start.

Diversity of living & non-living things Characteristics of living things Diversity of animals (vertebrates/invertebrates) Diversity of plants Diversity of materials Life cycles of animals (frogs, butterflies, mosquitoes, cockroaches) Life cycles of plants (flowering plants) Magnets — properties and uses
P4
Systems Introduced

P4 introduces the first "systems" — plant parts and human digestion — which require students to name parts, describe functions, AND explain how they work together. This is where CER writing becomes essential.

Plant system — parts and functions Parts of a flower (sepals, petals, stamens, pistil) Human digestive system — organs and functions Matter — solid, liquid, gas (properties and changes) Water cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation) Light — sources, shadows, reflection Heat — transfer, temperature, expansion
P5
Heaviest Content Year

P5 is the most content-dense Science year. Reproduction, water cycle details, two body systems, electrical systems, and photosynthesis all arrive at once. Note: 'Cells' was removed from P5 in 2023 — it is no longer tested.

Reproduction in plants (pollination, fertilisation, seed dispersal) Reproduction in animals Water cycle — detailed (transpiration, groundwater) Plant respiratory & circulatory systems Human respiratory system Human circulatory system Electrical system — circuits, switches, conductors/insulators Photosynthesis — conditions, reactants, products Energy conversion — forms and transformation
P6
PSLE Year

P6 Science is dominated by forces and ecology. PSLE open-ended questions on forces, food webs, and adaptation require precise vocabulary and the CER structure. "Air resistance" and "water resistance" as specific terms are de-emphasised from 2023 — the focus is on explaining effects of forces, not naming them.

Frictional force — effects and applications Gravitational force — weight vs mass Elastic spring force — compression and extension Interactions within the environment Adaptation — structural and behavioural Food chains and food webs Man's impact on the environment
Superholic Lab's Science Differentiator

The CER Method — What Examiners Are Looking For.

Most students know the content. The ones who lose marks write the right idea in the wrong structure. PSLE Science open-ended answers must follow Claim → Evidence → Reasoning to score full marks. Superholic Lab teaches this explicitly in every open-ended question.

C — Claim

A direct answer to the question. No hedging. No "I think." One clear statement of what happened or why.

Example:
"The plant in Pot A grew taller than the plant in Pot B."
E — Evidence

Data or observations from the question that support the claim. Must reference the specific information provided — not general knowledge.

Example:
"This is shown by the table — Pot A reached 24 cm while Pot B only reached 15 cm after 4 weeks."
R — Reasoning

The science concept that explains WHY the evidence supports the claim. This is where PSLE marks are won or lost — and where most students leave marks on the table.

Example:
"Pot A received more sunlight, which provided the energy needed for photosynthesis. More photosynthesis produced more glucose for growth."
Question: Why did the plant in Pot A grow taller than the plant in Pot B?
❌ 0 marks — Common student answer

"Because Pot A had more sunlight and sunlight helps plants grow."

No evidence from the table. No mechanism explained. Examiner cannot award marks — even though the idea is correct.

✓ 3 marks — CER structure

"Plant A grew taller because it received more sunlight. The table shows Pot A reached 24 cm while Pot B reached only 15 cm. Sunlight provides the energy for photosynthesis, which produces glucose for the plant to grow. With more sunlight, Pot A could carry out more photosynthesis and produce more glucose, resulting in greater growth."

Claim stated clearly. Evidence cited from the table. Reasoning links sunlight → photosynthesis → glucose → growth.

Superholic Lab teaches this CER structure in every open-ended Science question — and Miss Wena provides real-time Socratic guidance to help students build the reasoning themselves, rather than copying a model answer.

Sample MCQ — P4 Light

Which of the following best describes a shadow?

A) Light reflected off an object   B) A dark area formed when light is blocked   C) Light that passes through an object   D) The colour of an object when lit

Correct: B. Explanation: A shadow forms because light cannot pass through opaque objects — not because it reflects, which would produce a bright area, not a dark one.

Open-Ended — Booklet B
12–14 questions · 2–4 marks each · 44 marks total

Written answers using CER structure. These questions separate AL1 from AL3 — because knowledge alone is not enough. The answer must be structured, precise, and use MOE keyword language. Superholic Lab's marking scheme identifies missing Claim, missing Evidence, and missing Reasoning separately.

Sample Open-Ended — P6 Forces

Explain why a car slows down more quickly on a rough road than on a smooth road. (3 marks)

CER answer required: Claim (car slows faster on rough road), Evidence (rough surface texture), Reasoning (rough surface creates more frictional force opposing motion → car decelerates faster).

MOE 2023 Syllabus Update

Three Changes Parents Need to Know.

The current Primary Science Syllabus (implemented from 2023) introduced significant changes. Superholic Lab's question bank reflects the updated syllabus — not older content that is no longer tested.

'Cells' Removed from P5

The Cells topic has been removed from the Primary 5 Science syllabus. This was intentional — MOE wants students to focus on applying scientific concepts rather than memorising complex biological terminology. Any assessment book or resource that includes Cells at P5 is using an outdated syllabus.

No longer tested from 2023
Level-by-Level Syllabus Structure

Previously, P3 and P4 shared a "lower block" — schools could teach topics in any order. From 2024, every level has its own dedicated syllabus. All P4 students in Singapore now learn the same topics in the same sequence, regardless of school. This is better for revision preparation.

Standardised from 2024
Forces Terminology Simplified

P6 students no longer need to use the specific terms "air resistance" and "water resistance" when explaining force interactions. The focus has shifted to explaining the effects of forces — how they affect motion, direction, and speed — rather than naming force types. Superholic Lab's P6 questions reflect this updated emphasis.
